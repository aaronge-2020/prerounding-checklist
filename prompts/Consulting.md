# Consulting

Use this standard to prepare an inpatient specialty consultation request and a concise consult call. Work only from the de-identified information provided. Do not invent patient identifiers, findings, trends, diagnoses, tests, or consultant recommendations. If information is missing, say that it is not provided and list exactly what should be obtained before calling.

## Patient and situation

Clarify what the patient needs from the consultant:

* Further workup
* Management advice
* A procedure
* More than one of these

Before calling, assess from the available information whether the patient is stable enough for the consult or a possible procedure, consentable, able to participate in an interview, or in need of stabilization first. State unknown when the record does not answer one of these questions.

## Person

Identify the relevant body part, organ, or organ system and the consultant or specialty that should address the question. Use the patient name, MRN, and room only when they are present in the provided information; otherwise state that they are not provided.

## Timing and urgency

Classify the request as routine, urgent, or emergent. Routine generally means a response within about 24 hours. For routine requests, the best timing is during the week, ideally Monday through Friday from 8 AM to 4 PM, and not on the day of discharge when the question can safely wait. Do not delay an urgent or emergent consultation because of these scheduling preferences.

## Consult content

State the consult question immediately. A useful opening is: “I’m calling for a consult regarding …” When appropriate, label the request in the form “New routine consult for [problem or body system].”

The patient information should be limited to one sentence containing the age, pertinent history, and reason for admission. Follow it with a one-sentence brief hospital course that says what has happened. Then state why the consultant is being called now and the specific question the consultant should answer.

Be prepared to provide current vital signs, written as “V/S,” relevant labs, relevant imaging, and whether the patient is stable and consentable if a procedure may be needed.

## Condensed Consult Call Checklist

When calling a consult:

* Identify the patient: name, MRN, and room when available.
* Identify the relevant body system.
* State the consult question immediately: “I’m calling for a consult regarding …”
* Give a one-sentence patient summary: age, pertinent history, and why admitted.
* Give a brief one-sentence hospital course: what has happened.
* Explain why the consultant is being called now.
* Specify urgency: routine, about 24 hours; urgent; or emergent.
* Be ready to answer questions about V/S, relevant labs, imaging, and stability or consentability for a possible procedure.

# Pre-Rounding Bedside Checklist Instructions

You will be provided with the patient's **most recent progress notes, consultant notes, nursing notes, vital signs, laboratory results, imaging, microbiology, and other relevant diagnostic data.** Use this information to generate a focused bedside pre-rounding checklist.

The goal is to identify the **highest-yield questions to ask the patient and the focused physical exam maneuvers to perform** so that I can update today's assessment and plan. The checklist should be concise enough to complete in **2–5 minutes**.

---

# 1. Clinical Context (From the Chart)

Begin with a brief summary of:

* One-line patient summary (age, sex, relevant PMH, hospital day)
* Reason for hospitalization
* Active problems
* Current clinical trajectory (improving, worsening, unchanged)
* Significant overnight events
* New abnormal labs, imaging, microbiology, or consultant recommendations
* Pending studies or procedures

Do **not** ask the patient about information already available in the chart. Instead, use the chart to determine what information still needs to be clarified at the bedside.

---

# 2. Bedside Questions

Generate only questions that could meaningfully change today's management.

Include:

* How are you feeling compared to yesterday?
* What is bothering you most today?
* Any new symptoms or concerns overnight?
* Questions targeted to the patient's active diagnoses (e.g., dyspnea for heart failure, melena for GI bleed, dysuria for UTI, new weakness for stroke).

Do **not** generate a complete review of systems. Ask only diagnosis-specific, high-yield questions.

---

# 3. Functional Status

When relevant, assess:

* Ambulation
* Oral intake
* Urination
* Bowel movements
* Pain control
* Ability to perform baseline activities

---

# 4. Focused Physical Exam

Recommend only the physical exam maneuvers that are most likely to influence today's management.

Examples include:

* General appearance
* Mental status
* Heart
* Lungs
* Abdomen
* Extremities (edema, pulses)
* Neurologic exam
* Volume status
* Skin
* Wounds, drains, catheters, surgical sites

Tailor the exam to the patient's active problems. Do not recommend a routine head-to-toe exam.

---

# 5. Additional Checklist Based on Clinical Scenario

## If This Is a New Admission

Confirm:

* Allergies
* Home medications and adherence
* Baseline functional status
* Living situation
* Emergency contact/decision maker
* Code status
* Tobacco, alcohol, or drug use (when relevant)
* Patient's understanding of why they came to the hospital

## If Discharge Is Likely Within 24–48 Hours

Assess discharge readiness by asking:

* Do you feel ready to go home?
* Where will you be going after discharge?
* Do you have transportation?
* Will someone be available to help you?
* Do you need home health, PT/OT, oxygen, dialysis, wound care, or other services?
* Do you have your medications?
* Do you need refills?
* Any concerns about obtaining or paying for medications?
* Do you have transportation for follow-up appointments?
* Do you understand the discharge plan?

---

# 6. Output Goals

The bedside checklist should help me determine:

* Whether the patient is improving, worsening, or unchanged
* Whether the current diagnosis remains most likely or if new findings suggest an alternative diagnosis
* Whether the patient has responded to treatment
* Whether new complications have developed
* Whether today's plan should change
* Whether the patient is medically ready for discharge and what barriers remain

---

# General Rules

* Assume I have already reviewed the chart and provided all relevant notes, labs, vitals, and results.
* Do **not** ask questions that are already answered by the chart unless confirmation is clinically important.
* Prioritize questions and physical exam maneuvers that will change today's assessment, plan, or disposition.
* Tailor every question and exam maneuver to the patient's active diagnoses and current clinical course.
* Keep the entire bedside checklist concise enough to complete in **2–5 minutes**.
* Avoid generic review-of-systems questions and routine head-to-toe physical exams unless clinically indicated.

# Discharge Instructions Prompt

Write clear, patient-friendly discharge instructions at approximately a **6th–8th grade reading level**. The tone should be reassuring, concise, and free of unnecessary medical jargon. Explain *why* the patient was hospitalized, *what happened*, *what they need to do at home*, and *when to seek help*.

Organize the instructions using the following format.

---

# Dear [Patient Name],

Thank the patient for allowing the hospital to participate in their care.

## Why You Were in the Hospital

Write **2–4 short paragraphs** that explain:

* Why the patient came to the hospital.
* The major diagnosis(es).
* The important tests, procedures, or surgeries performed.
* Significant complications or important findings.
* How the patient's condition improved.
* Any important diagnoses that still require outpatient follow-up.

Avoid simply listing every test performed. Explain each test only if it changes the patient's understanding of their illness or future care.

---

# What You Should Do at Home

Provide clear, actionable bullet points.

Include only instructions relevant to this patient, such as:

* Medication changes
* Diet
* Activity restrictions
* Wound care
* Oxygen
* Dialysis
* Fluid restrictions
* Blood sugar monitoring
* Blood pressure monitoring
* Weight monitoring
* CPAP/BiPAP
* Other disease-specific instructions

Group instructions by disease when appropriate (e.g., Heart Failure, Diabetes, Dialysis).

---

# Medications

Briefly summarize major medication changes in plain language.

Examples:

* Start...
* Stop...
* Continue...
* Take as directed...
* Do not restart ___ unless instructed by your doctor.

Do **not** rewrite the complete medication reconciliation. Assume the official medication list will be attached separately.

---

# Follow-up Appointments

List follow-up by specialty.

For each provider include:

* Who to follow up with
* Recommended timeframe
* Purpose of the visit

Example:

* Primary Care (within 1 week): Hospital follow-up and medication review.
* Gastroenterology (2–4 weeks): Review endoscopy results.
* Cardiology (1 month): Evaluate elevated cardiac enzymes.

---

# When to Call Your Doctor

List symptoms requiring prompt outpatient evaluation.

Include only symptoms relevant to the patient's diagnoses.

---

# When to Go to the Emergency Department or Call 911

List red-flag symptoms requiring emergency evaluation.

Tailor these to the patient's hospitalization.

Examples:

* Chest pain
* Difficulty breathing
* Severe bleeding
* New confusion
* Passing out
* High fever
* Stroke symptoms

---

# Important Reminders

Include only clinically relevant reminders such as:

* Avoid NSAIDs
* Complete antibiotics
* Monitor daily weight
* Finish steroid taper
* Keep follow-up appointments
* Obtain recommended labs
* Tell future providers about this hospitalization

Do not include generic reminders that apply to every patient.

---

# Follow-up Testing

If applicable, include:

* Pending laboratory tests
* Pending pathology
* Pending cultures
* Imaging to be repeated
* Recommended outpatient procedures

Clearly explain:

* What is pending
* Who should follow up
* Approximate timing

---

# General Rules

* Write directly to the patient using "you."
* Use short paragraphs and bullet points.
* Explain medical conditions in plain language.
* Focus on what the patient needs to know after discharge.
* Avoid unnecessary medical terminology.
* Do not include hospital-specific phone numbers, addresses, or boilerplate text unless provided.
* Do not recreate the medication reconciliation; assume it will be attached automatically.
* Tailor every section to the patient's hospitalization rather than using generic discharge language.
* If multiple conditions were treated, organize instructions by condition to improve readability.
* If the patient underwent a procedure or surgery, include procedure-specific aftercare instructions.
* Ensure every recommendation has a clear purpose so the patient understands *why* it is important.

# Admission H&P Instructions

Write a concise, decision-focused admission H&P for an attending physician. The note should read as one coherent clinical presentation, not as a chart dump. Use only the supplied information. Return only the completed note.

## Core standard

The HPI tells the patient's story. Objective reports measured evidence. Assessment interprets that evidence. Plan states only documented decisions and actions. Put each fact in its owning section and do not repeat it elsewhere unless a brief reference is essential to the reasoning.

Before writing, silently establish:

1. the explicit admission date and the sequence before arrival, in the ED, and after admission;
2. the dominant reason for admission and the management-changing questions;
3. which statements are patient-reported, directly observed or measured, completed actions, active orders, planned actions, consultant recommendations, pending decisions, or unclear/conflicting;
4. which facts actually change diagnosis, severity, management, risk, or disposition.

If dates or sources conflict, use explicit dates and times when available. Otherwise say that the timing or status is unclear. Never repair a chronology by guessing. Do not infer that a planned action occurred, that a consultant evaluated the patient, or that a medication was administered unless the source documents it.

Do not add customary tests, treatments, diagnoses, thresholds, causal claims, consultant recommendations, follow-up, or discharge criteria from medical knowledge. A clinically sensible action is still prohibited unless it is documented in the supplied material.

## Required structure

Use exactly these top-level headings, in this order:

1. Chief Complaint
2. HPI
3. Past Medical History
4. Past Surgical History
5. Medications
6. Allergies
7. Family History
8. Social History
9. Review of Systems
10. Objective
11. Assessment
12. Plan
13. Disposition

Target a 3–5 minute spoken presentation. Finish every section, but omit irrelevant detail and empty boilerplate.

## Chief Complaint

Use one short patient-centered phrase.

## HPI

Begin with a one-sentence summary containing age, sex, the few relevant baseline conditions or recent procedures, and the reason for admission.

Then tell one chronological story in short paragraphs:

- baseline state and relevant context;
- symptom onset and evolution before arrival;
- the change or concern that prompted presentation;
- completed prehospital or ED treatment stated without measurements or test results;
- the patient's current symptom report, goals, or concerns when documented and relevant.

Use calendar dates or clear encounter phases when relative day labels could be confusing. Do not jump forward to the current inpatient state and then backward to symptom onset.

The HPI may contain only history, symptoms, meaningful negatives, relevant baseline function, and a brief statement of completed prehospital or ED actions. It must not contain:

- vital-sign values or claims derived from them;
- physical-examination findings;
- laboratory, microbiology, ECG, or imaging results;
- clinician interpretation or severity labels derived from objective data;
- consultant recommendations;
- planned tests, treatment, monitoring, or disposition;
- medication doses unless essential to the preadmission history.

If a sentence depends on a measurement, examination maneuver, or diagnostic test, move it to Objective. If it explains what the evidence means, move it to Assessment or the applicable Plan problem.

## Past Medical and Surgical History

Include only conditions and procedures that affect this admission, inpatient risk, medication safety, or disposition. Group related medical diagnoses by clinical category in compact lines. Include dates or severity only when documented and decision-relevant. Do not reproduce the full problem list or repeat treatment details that belong in Medications.

## Medications

List relevant preadmission medications by indication. Include dose, route, and frequency when documented. Keep ED and inpatient administrations out of this section. If medication lists conflict or the indication is unclear, state that reconciliation is required.

## Allergies, Family History, and Social History

Keep each concise. Do not infer missing reactions, substance use, family conditions, living arrangements, or functional status. Include social and functional details only when they affect the illness or disposition.

## Review of Systems

Include only patient-reported positives and meaningful negatives not already stated in the HPI. Do not repeat the HPI wholesale. Never place examination findings, vital signs, diagnostics, interpretation, or plans here.

## Objective

Use these subheadings:

### Vitals and Clinical Support

Summarize only decision-relevant admission and latest documented values. Prefer a compact sentence or the shortest meaningful trend; do not paste flowsheet tables. Time-qualify older values and do not call them current.

### Focused Physical Examination

Report only abnormal, changed, or management-relevant findings. Examination maneuvers—including tenderness, strength, range of motion, and bedside observations—belong here, not in HPI or Review of Systems.

### Key Labs and Diagnostics

Report only results that support or challenge the admission diagnosis, establish severity, change management, or remain pending. Summarize panels rather than reproducing them. Give timing sufficient to establish a real trend. Do not interpret results in this section.

## Assessment

In one or two sentences, synthesize the dominant reason for admission, current severity or trajectory when supported, and the principal management-changing uncertainty. Do not repeat the one-liner, list every diagnosis, or include orders.

## Plan

Organize active problems from highest to lowest priority. Include no more than three major problem groups unless another problem has a distinct admission decision.

For each problem, use:

### Problem name

**Assessment:** One to three sentences of chart-supported reasoning: current severity, leading interpretation, minimum supporting evidence, and the most important uncertainty. Label suspected diagnoses as suspected. Do not invent causation.

**Actions:** A short bullet list containing only actions explicitly documented as completed, ordered, continued, held, stopped, requested, recommended, planned, or pending. Preserve that status exactly. Do not turn a question into a recommendation or a usual practice into an order. Do not invent contingencies or numeric thresholds.

Medication administrations, holds, and substitutions belong under the problem they address. Distinguish an administration from an active order and a narrative plan. If plans conflict, identify the reconciliation issue rather than choosing one.

Omit incidental abnormalities and stable chronic problems that have no active admission decision. Do not create new outpatient workups or follow-up recommendations unless documented.

## Disposition

State only the documented anticipated destination and the specific barriers preventing discharge. If either is not documented, say so briefly. Do not create discharge criteria.

## Final silent check

Before returning the note, verify that:

- the HPI can be read aloud as one uninterrupted chronological story;
- no objective finding appears in HPI or Review of Systems;
- no fact is duplicated in detail across sections;
- explicit dates and encounter phases control the timeline;
- every Plan action is traceable to supplied information and retains its documented status;
- questions and pending decisions remain unresolved rather than being answered;
- no diagnosis, treatment, test, threshold, causation, consultation, or disposition criterion was invented.

If a management-changing fact is unavailable, conflicting, or temporally unclear, state that limitation instead of filling the gap.

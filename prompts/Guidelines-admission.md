# Concise H&P Presentation Instructions (3–5 Minutes)

Using only the provided information, generate a concise, decision-focused admission H&P for rapid inpatient presentation. Keep the H&P format; do not convert it to SOAP. The presentation should be comprehensive enough to include clinically relevant history while remaining deliverable in **3–5 minutes**. Prioritize information that changes the admission diagnosis, management, risk, or disposition.

Before writing, silently classify every supplied statement as presenting history, relevant baseline information, a documented completed action, a current order, a medication administration, a medication hold or discontinuation, a planned action, a consultant recommendation, a pending decision, a question for the team, conflicting information, or information whose timing cannot be established. Do not output this classification.

Do not convert background information, a planned discussion, a team question, or customary medical practice into a current recommendation. Do not infer that an action was completed merely because it was planned. Use consulted, consultation requested, team plans to consult, consultant recommended, and procedure completed only when the supplied source supports that exact status.

Use the admission history and ED course as the primary sources for the HPI. Place measured or observed findings in Objective. Do not reproduce prior notes, the full chart, or irrelevant remote history. If information is absent, omit it or state that it is not documented when the omission affects care; never reconstruct missing clinical data from memory or general medical knowledge.

Complete every required section before adding detail. Compress stable, chronic, resolved, remote, and background information before shortening the dominant active problem. Complete every sentence and bullet.

---

# Silent Chronology and Consistency Check

First establish the admission date and encounter sequence using explicit dates and times. Treat those anchors as authoritative. If another source uses an incompatible relative label such as today, yesterday, or hospital day one, do not copy or silently repair it. Use the explicit date or time when possible; otherwise state that the timing is unclear.

Create a silent event timeline for the presenting illness. Separate symptoms before arrival, ED findings and interventions, and subsequent inpatient events. Do not create a sequence from laboratory values merely because they appear in a particular order in the source.

For every transfusion mentioned, identify the documented administration time, the nearest pre-transfusion value, and the nearest post-transfusion value. If the number or timing of units is incomplete, report the known facts and state that the chronology is insufficient to assess the response. Do not call anemia transfusion refractory, an inappropriate response, or failure to respond unless clearly timed evidence supports that conclusion. Prefer continued transfusion requirement when repeated transfusions are documented but their response cannot be reliably calculated.

Create a separate silent medication ledger for each anticoagulant and antiplatelet medication. Track the drug name, indication when supplied, route, and whether it was administered, held, stopped, or merely proposed. Do not treat Eliquis, rivaroxaban, systemic heparin, prophylactic heparin, and hemodialysis catheter-lock heparin as interchangeable. When plans conflict, state the reconciliation problem rather than selecting a regimen.

---

# Source Priority

Give greatest weight to clearly timed admission or ED medication administrations, active orders, vital signs, laboratory results, bedside findings, diagnostic results, and consultant decisions. Use older information only to establish the presenting trajectory, relevant baseline conditions, important procedures or devices, medication indications, and the baseline state of unresolved problems.

Do not allow an older assessment or plan to override newer evidence. Do not describe an older oxygen requirement, vital sign, examination, medication regimen, or diagnostic result as current. When only the latest known value is available, identify it as the latest documented value and do not imply that it is current.

When sources conflict, do not silently reconcile them. Briefly state clinically important discrepancies and what requires confirmation. Distinguish a medication administered and later held from one held throughout the encounter. Distinguish a current order from an administration record and from a narrative plan.

Use only supplied information. Do not introduce diagnoses, causal claims, thresholds, tests, treatments, consultant recommendations, procedures, escalation criteria, medication restart timing, or contingencies from general medical knowledge. Temporal association alone does not establish causation.

---

# Relevance and Section Ownership

Include a fact only if it helps explain the presenting illness, establishes a meaningful baseline, changes diagnosis or management, affects risk, or influences disposition. Omit unrelated remote history, such as a healed minor injury that has no bearing on the current admission.

Place each fact in the section that owns that type of information. Avoid repeating detailed information across sections. A brief reference in the opening HPI summary is permitted when needed to orient the listener, but the full details belong in the designated section.

| Information | Primary section |
|---|---|
| Presenting symptoms, relevant baseline function, and prehospital or ED events | HPI |
| Diagnosis, severity, relevant sequelae, or status | Past Medical History |
| Procedure or operation and relevant date | Past Surgical History |
| Drug, dose, route, frequency, indication, and preadmission status | Medications |
| Social and functional factors | Social History |
| Measured, observed, or test-derived findings | Objective |
| Global illness severity and trajectory | Assessment |
| Problem-specific reasoning and differential | Plan, under the applicable problem |
| Orders, actions, recommendations, and pending decisions | Plan |

PMH states what the patient has; Medications states how it is treated. Do not include medication names in PMH or reproduce a diagnosis's full treatment history outside the relevant sections. Do not restate a surgery outside Past Surgical History unless the procedure itself is part of the presenting illness and is necessary for chronology.

---

# Required H&P Structure

Use exactly these sections, in this order, and do not add SOAP headings or unrequested top-level sections:

1. Chief Complaint
2. HPI
3. Past Medical History
4. Past Surgical History
5. Medications
6. Allergies
7. Family History
8. Social History
9. Review of Systems
10. Objective
11. Assessment
12. Plan
13. Disposition

Discharge Medications and Preventive Care should be included only when explicitly requested or when they contain an admission decision that cannot be placed in the relevant Plan problem.

---

# Chief Complaint

* State the chief complaint in one short phrase.

---

# HPI

* Begin with one concise 20–30-word summary containing:

  * Age
  * Sex
  * 2–4 most relevant PMH or recent procedures
  * Reason for admission
* Present the illness chronologically, emphasizing the change that prompted admission, its relevant context, and the decisions needed now.
* When applicable to the presenting symptom, incorporate the documented OPQRSTU elements: onset, provoking or palliating factors, quality, region or radiation, severity, timing or trajectory, and the patient's understanding or impact of the symptom. Do not force the mnemonic into the output, add undocumented details, or repeat information solely to complete every element.
* Include only patient-reported history and symptoms, pertinent positives and meaningful negatives, relevant baseline function, and completed prehospital or ED events that affect inpatient care.
* Include the patient's goals or concerns only when they influence decisions.
* Exclude unnecessary ROS, vital signs, physical-exam findings, laboratory values, imaging details, clinician interpretation, consultant recommendations, and treatment plans.

Do not include medication doses, prior test results, or resolved diagnoses in the HPI unless they are necessary to understand the presenting illness.

Do not imply that a planned action occurred or that an undocumented treatment response was observed. A patient-reported event may appear here; its measured consequences belong in Objective or Plan.

---

# Past Medical History

* List only diagnoses relevant to the current admission, inpatient decisions, medication safety, risk, or disposition. Omit remote conditions that have no current clinical relevance.
* **Group related diagnoses under one clinical category** instead of listing each on its own line. Use categories such as Cardiovascular, Pulmonary, Endocrine/Metabolic, Renal, GI/Hepatic, Neuro/Psych, Oncologic, Rheum/MSK, etc. — only include categories that apply.
* Within a category, state the diagnoses in a single line, including only qualifiers that matter clinically (severity, key dates, relevant sequelae).

Example:

**Cardiovascular:** HTN, HLD, paroxysmal AFib (CHA₂DS₂-VASc 3), CAD s/p DES to LAD (2021)
**Endocrine:** T2DM (last A1c 8.2%), hypothyroidism
**Oncologic:** Prostate cancer, Gleason 7, diagnosed 2023

* If a diagnosis doesn't fit a shared category or stands alone, list it under its own single-item category rather than forcing a grouping.

Do not reproduce the complete historical problem list.

---

# Past Surgical History

* List only surgeries relevant to the presenting illness, current anatomy, procedural risk, medication decisions, or inpatient care (procedure + year or date when documented). Omit remote surgeries that do not affect the current admission. Do not restate the diagnosis that prompted the surgery if it is already covered in PMH.

---

# Medications

**Organize medications by disease rather than listing them alphabetically.** Use the diagnosis as a heading only — do not restate diagnosis details already given in PMH (e.g., severity, dates, staging).

Example:

**Hypertension**

* Amlodipine 10 mg daily
* Losartan 50 mg daily

**Type 2 Diabetes**

* Metformin 1000 mg BID
* Empagliflozin 10 mg daily

**Atrial Fibrillation**

* Apixaban 5 mg BID
* Metoprolol succinate 50 mg daily

Include dose, route, and frequency when documented. If a medication's indication is unclear or available lists conflict, state that reconciliation is required rather than assigning an indication or choosing a regimen.

Distinguish preadmission medications from ED or inpatient administrations. Admission medication holds, discontinuations, substitutions, and other decisions belong under the relevant problem in Plan.

Do not reproduce the complete medication administration record or include medications unrelated to current care unless they materially affect safety, interactions, withdrawal risk, or disposition.

---

# Allergies

* Drug or substance allergy → documented reaction
* Keep to one line when possible.

Do not infer a reaction. If no allergy information is supplied and the absence matters, state that allergies are not documented.

---

# Family History

* One sentence.
* Include only diseases relevant to current illness or hereditary risk.
* If the supplied history is noncontributory or unavailable, say so briefly without inventing detail.

---

# Social History

One concise sentence including only:

* Tobacco
* Alcohol
* Recreational drugs
* Living situation
* Functional status (if relevant)

Include occupational or exposure history only when relevant to the presenting illness. Do not repeat social or functional information elsewhere unless it constitutes a specific disposition barrier.

---

# Review of Systems

* Only pertinent positives and major negatives.
* Avoid exhaustive review.

Do not include examination findings, vital signs, laboratory values, imaging, clinician interpretation, or treatment plans in Review of Systems. Do not include routine appetite, sleep, voiding, or functional details unless they affect admission decisions.

---

# Objective

## Vitals and Clinical Support

* Include clinically relevant admission vital signs, oxygen requirement, or other support needs.
* When applicable, include the current value, direction of change, and relevant baseline or prior value.

Time-qualify each status. Do not describe an older measurement as current. If clinically important current vital signs or support requirements are unavailable, say so directly.

## Focused Physical Examination

Report only new, abnormal, or management-relevant findings organized by system:

* General
* Cardiovascular
* Pulmonary
* Abdomen
* Neurologic
* Extremities
* Other pertinent systems

Do not document a completely normal exam unless clinically relevant.

Findings elicited by palpation, movement, range-of-motion testing, strength testing, or another examination maneuver belong here rather than in the HPI or Review of Systems.

## Key Labs/Diagnostics

Include only laboratory values, ECG, imaging, microbiology, or other diagnostics whose current value, trend, or comparison with baseline changes or supports the admission diagnosis, assessment, or management.

When a comparison is clinically relevant, give the current value, direction of change, and baseline or prior value. Give the shortest meaningful trend with enough timing to prevent a false sequence. Do not infer chronology from the order in which results appear. Omit findings that are expected for the patient's known condition or planned treatment and do not alter a clinical decision.

Do not judge a transfusion response unless the transfusion and follow-up measurement are clearly timed. If the source reports a total number of units without complete timing, preserve that limitation.

Report findings objectively without interpretation. Do not include patient statements, assessment, or treatment plans.

Do not include a complete system-by-system examination, historical vital-sign tables, full laboratory panels, old microbiology reports, line age, catheter age, type-and-screen expiration, or routine normal findings unless they change admission management. Include only the current value and the most relevant trend, baseline, threshold, or comparison.

---

# Assessment

Use this section only for a brief global admission synthesis. In one or two concise sentences, state the patient's overall illness severity, current trajectory, dominant problem driving admission, and principal management-changing uncertainty.

Use improving, worsening, unchanged, stable, or tenuous only when the available evidence supports that term. Distinguish persistence of disease activity from physiologic stability. Do not call the patient hemodynamically stable when current vital signs are unavailable or when the sources materially disagree. Do not state an unlocalized suspected diagnosis as established or assign causation based only on temporal association.

Do not provide separate problem-by-problem assessments here; put each problem's clinical reasoning under that problem in Plan. Do not place orders, medication changes, monitoring instructions, consultation requests, thresholds, or contingencies in Assessment.

---

# Plan

Prioritize problems from most to least important. Combine related diagnoses when they lead to the same decisions, but do not assert an unsupported causal relationship.

## Acute Problems

For **each acute problem** use the following structure:

### Problem Name

**Assessment:** Explain what is happening, current severity and trajectory, the leading chart-supported diagnosis or interpretation, the most relevant competing explanation or uncertainty if it would change management, the minimum supporting evidence, and why it matters for admission. Use 2–3 sentences for the dominant problem, 1–2 sentences for other active problems, and one sentence for stable problems only when they affect care. Do not repeat the entire HPI or Objective section and do not make unsupported causal claims.

When a diagnosis remains suspected rather than established, use language such as suspected, possible, or not localized. Do not claim that infection, treatment failure, hemolysis, a consumptive process, or another explanation has been excluded unless the complete supplied evidence supports that conclusion.

**Actions:**

After the problem-specific Assessment, state the primary action or decision first. Use only action subheadings containing a necessary action; do not force every category. For the highest-priority problem, include no more than three action bullets. For other active problems, include no more than one or two action bullets:

## Action-Indication Gate

The Plan is not a recommendation engine. Include an action only when its **problem**, **indication**, and **purpose** are clear from the provided source: what active problem it addresses, what documented finding/order/recommendation/change makes it necessary now, and what decision, risk, symptom, uncertainty, or disposition barrier it addresses. If any element is unclear, omit the action.

Do not turn possibilities into recommendations. Do not use “consider,” “may benefit from,” “could obtain,” “would recommend,” or “should evaluate” unless the source explicitly documents that recommendation. Do not add routine tests, imaging, labs, consultations, procedures, monitoring, prophylaxis, medication changes, or guideline-based care merely because they are customary. Include them only when documented, ordered, explicitly recommended, or necessary to resolve a stated management-changing uncertainty.

Include a contingency only when both its trigger and response are supported by the source. Do not invent numeric thresholds, escalation criteria, transfusion targets, or ICU-transfer criteria. When the next action is not specified, state the uncertainty rather than inventing a plan.

Preserve action status precisely. Distinguish a completed intervention from a planned one, a current order from documented administration, and a medication administered and later held from one held throughout the encounter. If the team plans to involve a consultant but placement of the consultation is not documented, say that the team plans to involve the consultant and that consultation status is pending. Use consultation requested, consulted, or consultant recommended only when the source establishes that stage. Do not turn a question for the team into a recommendation; identify the pending decision instead.

* **Treatment/Medications:** Medications organized under the disease they treat, with dose, route, frequency, and indication; and relevant supportive care.
* **Diagnostics:** Initial or follow-up laboratory studies, imaging, microbiology, or other diagnostic studies.
* **Monitoring:** Clinical monitoring, vital-sign goals, intake/output, trending labs, or response-to-treatment checks.
* **Consults/Procedures:** Consultant recommendations, requested consultations, and planned procedures.
* **Contingencies:** If/then escalation or de-escalation plans when appropriate.
* **Discharge Needs:** Problem-specific discharge readiness, medication decisions, follow-up, services, or barriers when relevant.

Keep bullets short and actionable. Place each medication change, consultation, and escalation decision under one primary problem only; do not repeat the same action under multiple problems. Do not cite guidelines unless specifically requested.

## Medication Accuracy in the Plan

Place medication decisions under the problem they address. State medication status precisely, including when a medication was administered and subsequently held. Include dose, route, and frequency when they affect the admission decision or are necessary to communicate a documented action. Do not reproduce the full medication administration record.

When the chart contains incompatible anticoagulant, antiplatelet, or other medication plans, identify the exact reconciliation issue instead of choosing a regimen. Do not interpret dialysis-catheter heparin as systemic anticoagulation.

---

## Chronic Problems

For each condition:

* One concise interpretation of current status if it affects admission care
* Inpatient medication(s) only when active management or adjustment is needed
* Any necessary adjustment

Do not provide extensive discussion if stable; summarize stable conditions in one line or omit them.

Do not create separate problems solely to repeat routine home medications, completed treatment courses, unchanged devices, expected chronic laboratory abnormalities, or unrelated historical diagnoses. If an upcoming procedure could affect current management but its status is unclear, identify it as a coordination question rather than inventing a plan.

---

## Preventive Care *(Only if explicitly requested or admission-relevant; otherwise do not output as a separate section)*

Include only if applicable:

* DVT prophylaxis
* GI prophylaxis
* Vaccinations
* Code status

---

# Disposition

Provide one concise sentence stating the documented anticipated destination and the specific medical, functional, or social barriers preventing discharge. Base it on:

* Clinical trajectory
* Hemodynamic stability
* Remaining inpatient needs
* Rehabilitation/PT/OT needs
* Social support, living situation, and transport situation
* Whether the patient's care can be safely managed outside the hospital

Do not invent a destination. Do not convert every unresolved problem into a discharge barrier. Include only barriers documented in the source or directly established by the admission facts.

## Discharge Medications *(Only if explicitly requested; otherwise do not generate)*

List only medications requiring a discharge decision. Do **not** recreate the full medication reconciliation.

Organize into four categories:

* **Continue:** Home medications to continue unchanged.
* **Start:** New medications (include dose, frequency, duration if applicable, and indication).
* **Change:** Home medications with dose/frequency changes (briefly state why).
* **Hold/Stop:** Medications to discontinue or temporarily hold (include reason and restart instructions if known).

Finally, note any documented outpatient medication monitoring or patient counseling.

Keep this section brief and focused on medications that require action at discharge. Do not invent restart timing, laboratory schedules, or counseling requirements.

---

# Final Verification *(Internal guidance; do not output as a separate section)*

Before finalizing, verify that:

* All required H&P sections are present and in the specified order.
* Explicit dates and times drive the chronology; presenting history, ED events, and inpatient events are not conflated.
* Current and historical findings are not conflated.
* The HPI contains history and symptoms only; objective findings appear only in Objective.
* Clinically relevant PMH, surgical history, medications, allergies, family history, social history, and baseline function were retained.
* Unrelated remote diagnoses, procedures, medications, and incidental findings were omitted.
* Related PMH and medications are organized into clinically coherent groups.
* The top-level Assessment contains only global illness synthesis.
* Each active problem has its own clinical Assessment before its Actions in Plan.
* Every Plan action is supported by the provided source.
* Every Plan action passes the problem–indication–purpose gate; delete any action that does not.
* No unsupported guideline, threshold, diagnosis, order, or management recommendation was added.
* The transfusion chronology supports any statement about response.
* Medication events reflect their documented timestamps, routes, and status; catheter heparin was not mistaken for systemic anticoagulation.
* Consultant involvement is described at its documented stage, and questions or pending decisions remain unresolved rather than becoming recommendations.
* Preventive Care and Discharge Medications are omitted unless explicitly requested or admission-relevant.
* The H&P ends with a complete Disposition sentence and does not end mid-sentence or mid-bullet.
* Every included sentence changes the admission assessment, management, risk, or disposition.

If the supplied information is inadequate to determine a management-changing fact, state that it is unavailable, unclear, conflicting, or requires confirmation. Do not fill the gap with customary care.


---

# General Style Rules

* Keep the presentation concise enough to deliver in **3–5 minutes**.
* Eliminate unnecessary repetition between sections and keep full details in the owning section.
* Group related items within a section (e.g., cluster comorbidities by organ system in PMH, cluster medications by disease) rather than listing each item as its own line.
* Use headings and consistent formatting.
* Organize medications under the disease they treat.
* Include only information that explains the presenting illness, establishes a meaningful baseline, or changes diagnosis, management, risk, or disposition.
* Avoid unnecessary normal findings and exhaustive review of systems.
* Write in a style appropriate for attending rounds, assuming the audience has limited attention and wants only high-yield information.
